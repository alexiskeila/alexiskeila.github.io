Second Wave  by Alexis Viera

 

She had never been a greedy woman, but she felt it was long passed the time for modesty now.

 

Lilly’s eyes scoured the pages, bulging with gluttony in their crusade for complete enfranchisement. Each word morphed as she read it so that its angles might merge to form a sticky cloak, augmenting her unbearably wearisome life with a layer of complexity, a spectral mold of her femininity. She fingered the few remaining pages, already fantasizing about the deliverance that awaits in the volume’s conclusion, Simone de Beauvoir’s final, critical advice in shepherding her, a trite little sheep, to manumission.

 

Never had Lilly been so greedy, yet neither had she ever been so blissful in all her life. Even birthing her son produced a fraction of this sincere emotion, she thought—yes, certainly, it is better to be assured in one’s own value and competence than to bear a new life, especially a boy’s, whose character is yet unresolved, and more likely than not to leave one disheartened. Feminism, Lilly decided, was a religion, The Second Sex, its manifesto, and she, a newly enlightened disciple, entirely willing to follow her people through walls of water that might collapse at a moment of misfired thought. She read the final chapter breathlessly though she made no real sound, no sound but the low murmur of fingers along the book’s binding, along the etched title that spelt out her most profound desires, sweat exuding from the convolutions of her brain. She was done in the time it took to say a bedtime prayer.

 

A moment later, her husband’s mighty footfall ascending the stairs reverberated within her like a jagged splinter punctuating her ripe intellect. She braced herself for the sound of his voice, resembling more with each passing day a novice violinist whose bow had broken, but who continues to play, not out of love for the art form, but out of love for hearing himself.

 

“Lillian, why on earth are you up here with a stew simmering downstairs? I’m surprised I didn’t arrive to find the pad up in flames.”

 

Lilly patted down her skirt, wrinkled by the weight of her absorption, her lovely book safe in the night-drawer. She never thought to expend any sincere effort in stowing it away, for she knew he never assumed her life to be of any interest to him, a man of business. “Why, honey, I just came up for a second now to recall a Bible verse whose final line I couldn’t quite remember. Awful timing is all it was.”

 

“I should damn sure hope so. We’ve got a precious cargo in this house.” Lilly knew, as always, he was referring to three things: their son (who was identical to him despite her best efforts to mother him properly), his “library” (as it was obligingly referred to, to stoke his rampant pseudo-intellectuality, although it was a mere handful of shelves filled with books off the literary canon he was always “planning to read”), and his collection of ships-in-a-bottle (which he, admittedly, was quite talented at manufacturing, and was often the topic of dinner party conversation, although the best and most spectacular one was bought in an auction, which Lilly was never to divulge to guests).

 

Since beginning The Second Sex, she never quite understood why she hadn’t hated him all along—that’s not to say that she had loved him, but her feelings for him were always somewhere on the spectrum near the cooler end of lukewarm.  She married him on her parents’ insistence. Since she had a crooked nose and a left eye notably smaller than the right, no one ever bothered fooling themselves with the prospect of a marital life for her, so when Dale came along expressing interest in being a suitor, it didn’t so much matter whether she fancied him or not, but that he fancied her well enough. Neither could she gather why he had decided to marry her after all. She guessed he wished for an ugly spouse, over which he could exercise multiple levels of dominance (though truthfully, he had been rejected by four potential brides prior to their own engagement, and had been informed by a mutual friend that sons run in her family).

 

“Now, I’ve got something to tell you, Lillian, and I don’t want you to rattle your cage over any of it. It’s just a temporary situation, is all, just a bit of a bump in the road.”

 

“Alright then, get on with it,” Lilly said, hardly paying any mind to him at all. She was all too taken up with the merciful Lord who inflicted measles upon Madame Krüdener, a focus of one of the concluding chapters, with whom she experienced a rich sense of kinship.

 

“Well,” said Dale, rubbing his hands together in hopes of dissolving his stress into imperceptible particles with the friction. “Boss let me go today. Said they gotta cut back costs on marketing ‘cause it’s costing more than what it’s worth. I really don’t know how that can be, see, ‘cause I did some calculations and the figures show that—” He went on rambling his justifications, meticulously thought out, “Don’t have a crow now, Lillian—” although the assurance was not intended to soothe her, rather a hopeful articulation of his dejected circumstance, meant to boost his own spirits. Lilly didn’t mind, really. In fact, she hardly noticed he went on babbling after the first couple of words, had since then been reflecting upon this case of measles that a merciful Lord seemed to have been handing to her.
